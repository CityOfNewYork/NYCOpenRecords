What does FOIL mean?

FOIL stands for the Freedom of Information Law. The New York State Freedom of Information Law requires government agencies to provide records to the public upon request. Government records affect the lives of each New Yorker, and providing easy access to the records makes government work better.

What kinds of records do I have the right to see?

The FOIL definition of records is very broad and includes information found in paper and electronic documents and audio and visual recordings. All records are available upon request, unless an exception in FOIL permits an agency to deny access to a record.

Are there any public records that I can’t see?

Yes, there are exceptions in FOIL. Most of the exceptions are based upon common sense and the potential for harm that would arise if the contents of the record were disclosed to unauthorized people. If disclosure of a record would be damaging to an individual or prevent a government agency from carrying out its duties, it is likely that some or all of the record may be withheld. You can read the law, including the exceptions, by clicking here. See FOIL §87(2)

Do I have to explain why I want the records?

No, unless you are a business or other organization that is requesting a list of names and addresses.

How much does it cost to get access to government records?

There is no charge to submit a FOIL request or to look at records in the office of an agency. However, you may be charged by an agency for the cost of reproducing the records you requested. FOIL limits the amount that can be charged. You may always include in your request letter a specific statement limiting the amount that you are willing to pay. If an agency estimates that the total cost will exceed that amount, you may narrow your request in order to reduce the cost.

How long does it take to get access to government records?

The agency will acknowledge your request within five (5) business days of receiving it. The actual time to complete a request will vary depending on many factors, such as the number of records requested and the difficulty involved in locating and reviewing the records. You will be notified of the amount of time the agency needs to complete your request.

Can I make an anonymous request?

On the OpenRecords portal, your name will not be viewable by the public. The topic of the request will be shown.

How do I know which agency handles the records I need?

The OpenRecords portal has a field named “categories” that will help you identify the correct agency. When you choose one of the categories, the list of possible agencies will be narrowed to those that may have the records you want. You can select the agency from the list provided to start the FOIL request process. If you submit a records request to the wrong agency, you will be notified within five days that the request should be resubmitted to a different agency.

I asked for a copy of public records and the agency refused my request, saying that it was too broad. What can I do?

An agency may deny a request that does not "reasonably describe" records. If the request is too vague, the agency may ask for clarification. You may then submit a new request with a more specific description of the records you would like.

How do I track a request?

After your request has been submitted, a message will display verifying the submission and providing a request number. Select Search Requests from the top menu and enter your request number, then press the Search button. The status of your request will be displayed.

What if I have multiple requests that I want to track or would like to see what other requests have been submitted?

Select View Requests from the top menu. You can then search existing requests using text from the request, the current status, the date range and the agency.

What if I don’t agree with an agency’s response?

You may appeal a decision to deny your request within 30 days of receiving the denial or partial denial. The denial or partial denial should provide the contact information for the Agency Appeals Officer. Your appeal letter should state the reason you are appealing and why the agency's response to the request was improper.

How does OpenRecords calculate dates for the 5 day acknowledgement period for a request and the response time for an appeal?

Any request or appeal that comes in electronically between midnight and midnight on a weekday is counted by OpenRecords as received that day, and the time period begins to run the next day the office is open.


Examples:

  • For requests received through the portal Monday 1 AM, 5 AM, 9 AM, 4:30 PM, 8 PM, or 11:59 PM, OpenRecords would calculate the due date for acknowledgment as the following Monday.
  • Responses to appeals received through the portal Monday 1 AM, 5 AM, 9 AM, 4:30 PM, 8 PM, or 11:59PM, OpenRecords would calculate the due date for acknowledgment as two Mondays later.
  • If a request is received through the portal any time on the day before a holiday, the period for acknowledgment begins to run the next work day. If a request is received any time on Wednesday and the next day, Thursday, is a holiday, the period for acknowledgment would begin to run on Friday and OpenRecords would calculate the due date for acknowledgment as the following Thursday.

What if a request or appeal is submitted electronically over the weekend? How does OpenRecords calculate the 5 day acknowledgement period or the appeal response time?

Any request or appeal received electronically over the weekend is counted by OpenRecords as received that day, and the 5-day acknowledgement period or the appeal response time begins to run the next day the agency is open.


Examples:

  • OpenRecords calculates the due date for acknowledgment for requests received through the portal any time on a Friday as due by the end of day on the next Friday.
  • OpenRecords calculates the due date for acknowledgment for requests received through the portal on Saturday or Sunday, at any time, as due by end of day on Friday.
  • OpenRecords calculates the due date for acknowledgment for requests received through the portal on Monday at 1 AM as due by the end of day on the next Monday.

What if a request is submitted to OpenRecords on a holiday? How does OpenRecords calculate the 5 day acknowledgement period or the appeal response time?

The acknowledgement period or appeal response time for any request or appeal received through OpenRecords on a holiday begins to run the next day the office is open.


Examples:

  • Request is received through the portal on Thursday, Christmas Day. The acknowledgement period begins to run on Friday and OpenRecords calculates the due date for acknowledgment as the following Thursday.
  • A request is received by through the portal on Friday, New Year’s Day. The acknowledgement period begins to run the following Monday and OpenRecords calculates the due date for acknowledgment as the following Friday.
